My Story

I never thought I'd end up as a developer. Back in college, I was studying mechanical engineering at K.L. University, drawing machine parts and calculating stress factors. But during my third year, I stumbled upon a Data Structures and Algorithms course that changed everything. I was hooked.

While my classmates were designing engines, I was staying up late teaching myself to code. It paid off when I became the only mechanical engineering student in my class to land a software developer job after graduation. Looking back, I'm grateful for that mechanical background—it gave me a unique way of breaking down complex problems.

These days, I work at Beamx Techlabs, where I've grown from writing my first professional code to leading projects and designing systems. The journey hasn't always been smooth—I still remember staring at my screen at 2 AM, trying to debug a particularly nasty issue with a knowledge graph query. But those moments of struggle are what make the breakthroughs so satisfying.

What Drives Me

share_reviews Knowledge Graphs

I fell in love with knowledge graphs the first time I saw how they connect information in ways that just make sense. There's something almost magical about watching relationships between data points reveal insights that would stay hidden in traditional databases. I've spent countless hours experimenting with different graph schemas, trying to map out everything from movie recommendations to complex business domains.

lan Distributed Systems

I'm fascinated by how distributed systems work—these complex networks of services that somehow manage to work together despite all the things that could go wrong. Working with serverless architectures has been like solving an ever-evolving puzzle. How do you make sure everything stays consistent? What happens when something fails? These questions keep me up at night (in a good way).

search Search & Discovery

I believe search is far more than just a box you type into—it's the bridge between people and information. A good search experience feels almost like mind-reading, anticipating what users need before they fully express it. I've spent hours fine-tuning search algorithms, trying to find that perfect balance between relevance, performance, and surprise (because sometimes the best results are ones you weren't explicitly looking for).

I want to build technology that feels approachable to everyone—not just experts. Coming from a non-traditional background myself, I know firsthand how important it is to break down those barriers.

What I've Learned

Learning Never Stops

When I switched from mechanical engineering to coding, I thought I just needed to learn a few languages and frameworks. I couldn't have been more wrong. Five years later, I'm still learning something new almost daily. I've realized that knowing how to learn efficiently is far more valuable than any specific knowledge. Every new technology I pick up gives me a fresh perspective on problems I thought I already understood.

Good Tech Feels Invisible

The best technologies are ones you barely notice—they just work. I try to bring this principle to everything I build. Coming from a self-taught background made me appreciate well-written documentation and intuitive interfaces. There's a special joy in creating something complex that feels simple to use, whether it's an API that other developers love working with or an application that makes a complicated process feel effortless.

The most rewarding moments in my career haven't been about the technologies I've used, but the problems I've helped solve and the people I've helped along the way.
