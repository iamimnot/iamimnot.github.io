My Journey

My love affair with computers began in childhood, but my path wasn't straightforward. I studied mechanical engineering at K.L. University, where options for computer science were limited in my region. It was during my third year when I discovered a specialized course in Data Structures and Algorithms that changed everything.

Working twice as hard as my classmates, I became the only mechanical engineering student to secure a software developer position after graduation. This transition from mechanical engineering to becoming a self-taught developer has shaped how I approach problems—with a blend of analytical mechanical thinking and creative coding solutions.

I still remember the exact moment when I realized my self-taught knowledge wasn't enough. It was late at night at Beamx Techlabs, struggling with optimizing queries for a knowledge graph application. Despite hours researching online and trying different approaches, the solution remained elusive. This humbling experience became a turning point in my journey.

Now, I'm constantly exploring the boundaries of my knowledge, particularly in graph databases, distributed systems, and artificial intelligence. Each challenge has become an opportunity to delve deeper into the theoretical foundations that underpin our technological world.

Learning Through Building

W6H: A Journey into Knowledge Graphs

This project began with a simple question: how can we connect structured and unstructured data in more intuitive ways? Using Rust's Axum framework, I built a RESTful API that bridged Apache AGE for graph relationships with CouchDB for document storage.

The challenge of learning multiple database paradigms simultaneously and developing efficient cross-database queries pushed me to my limits. When the system successfully scaled to handle over 10,000 daily requests, it was both a professional achievement and a personal revelation about the power of graph-based data models.

Rust Axum Apache AGE CouchDB MeiliSearch

Axiom Cloud: Lessons in Distributed Systems

What started as a simple email service evolved into a fascinating exploration of asynchronous processing. I designed a serverless architecture using AWS Lambda, SQS, S3, and SES that elegantly handled unpredictable traffic spikes—something I once would have approached with traditional scaling.

This project taught me valuable lessons about distributed systems: how components communicate, how to manage state across services, and how to design for failure. Each optimization revealed deeper questions about theoretical concepts I'm now eager to explore more formally.

AWS Lambda SQS S3 SES Python

Knowledge Graph POC: From Theory to Practice

This experimental project allowed me to implement complex graph schemas in a practical context. By adapting the Cars schema from Schema.org, I created a network of nodes and relationships that represented automotive data in ways traditional databases couldn't match.

The most rewarding aspect was seeing how naturally graph databases model complex real-world relationships. The challenges I faced with query optimization highlighted the gap between practical implementations and theoretical understanding that I'm working to bridge.
